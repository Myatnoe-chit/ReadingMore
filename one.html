1984

by George Orwell

Overview

"1984" is a dystopian social science fiction novel and cautionary tale, written by the English writer George Orwell. It was published on 8 June 1949 by Secker & Warburg as Orwell's ninth and final book completed in his lifetime. The story is set in a totalitarian society ruled by the Party and its leader, Big Brother, who employ surveillance and mind control to maintain power.

About the Author

George Orwell, born Eric Arthur Blair, was an English novelist, essayist, journalist, and critic. He is best known for his novels "Animal Farm" and "1984," which have become defining works of dystopian literature. Orwell was a man of strong opinions who addressed some of the major political movements of his time, including imperialism, fascism, and communism.

Key Themes

  • Totalitarianism: The novel explores the dangers of a totalitarian government that exercises complete control over its citizens.
  • Surveillance: The concept of constant surveillance by the government, epitomized by Big Brother, is a central theme.
  • Manipulation of Truth: The Party controls information, history, and reality, manipulating the truth to maintain power.
  • Individual vs. State: The novel portrays the struggle of the individual against the oppressive power of the state.
